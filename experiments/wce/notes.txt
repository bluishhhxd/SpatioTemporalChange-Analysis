# 📝 **Weighted Cross Entropy (WCE) — Notes for Analysis**

## **1. Motivation**

Weighted Cross Entropy (WCE) was introduced to address **severe class imbalance** in the semantic change detection task.

The dataset exhibits a highly skewed distribution of transition classes, where a small subset of transitions (e.g., no-change or dominant land-cover types) accounts for the vast majority of pixels, while many classes appear extremely rarely.

To mitigate this imbalance, class weights were incorporated into the loss function to increase the contribution of rare classes during training.

---

## **2. Weighting Strategy**

Class weights were computed based on inverse logarithmic scaling of class frequencies:

[
w_i = \frac{1}{\log(\text{count}_i + 1.1)}
]

The weights were then normalized:

[
w_i = \frac{w_i}{\text{mean}(w)}
]

### **Rationale**

* Log scaling prevents extreme explosion of weights for very rare classes
* Normalization ensures numerical stability during training
* Avoids aggressive suppression of frequent classes (compared to inverse frequency)

---

## **3. Observations During Training**

* Training remained stable across epochs
* Loss decreased consistently
* Accuracy improved steadily (~0.61 → ~0.76)
* IoU and F1 scores showed moderate improvement

This indicates that the model was successfully learning dominant patterns in the dataset.

---

## **4. Per-Class Performance Analysis (Key Insight)**

Despite stable training behavior, per-class accuracy revealed:

* High performance on dominant classes (e.g., classes with extremely high pixel counts)
* Near-zero accuracy on most rare classes

This indicates that:

```text
The model remained biased toward frequent classes despite weighting.
```

---

## **5. Why WCE Was Insufficient**

Although WCE increased the relative importance of rare classes, it did not sufficiently counteract the extreme imbalance in the dataset.

### **Key Limitation**

The imbalance in pixel counts spans several orders of magnitude:

```text
Frequent classes: ~10^9 – 10^10 pixels  
Rare classes: ~10^4 – 10^6 pixels
```

Even after weighting:

* Frequent classes still dominate the overall loss
* Rare classes contribute negligibly to gradient updates

---

### **Mathematical Insight**

Loss contribution is proportional to:

[
\text{Contribution}_i \propto \text{count}_i \times w_i
]

Thus:

* Frequent classes still produce significantly larger gradients
* Rare classes remain under-optimized

---

## **6. Effect on Model Behavior**

The model converges to a solution where:

* Predictions are dominated by high-frequency classes
* Rare transitions are effectively ignored
* Overall accuracy remains high due to class imbalance

This leads to misleading performance metrics when evaluated globally.

---

## **7. Conclusion**

Weighted Cross Entropy improves training stability but is insufficient for handling **extreme class imbalance** in semantic change detection tasks.

It fails to:

* Ensure adequate learning for rare classes
* Balance gradient contributions effectively

---

## **8. Transition to Next Approach**

Due to the limitations observed with WCE, more advanced loss functions were explored:

* Focal Loss (to emphasize hard examples)
* Dice-based losses (to improve overlap for minority classes)
* Tversky variants (to explicitly control false positives/negatives)
