🧾 Analysis: Weighted Focal Loss (WFL)


1. Overall Performance
The model trained using Weighted Focal Loss (WFL) achieves:
Accuracy: ~0.91
IoU: ~0.60
F1 Score: ~0.67 (best at Epoch 2)

Compared to previous loss functions:
CE → F1 ~0.53
WCE → F1 ~0.63
FL → F1 ~0.67
WFL → F1 ~0.67 (comparable to FL)

This indicates that WFL maintains strong overall segmentation performance.


2. Class-wise Performance
The per-class results show an unexpected behavior:
Class 7: Acc ≈ 0.9996
Class 13: Acc ≈ 0.0027
Class 14: Acc = 0.0000
Most other classes: 0.0000

A notable observation is that Class 14, which was previously one of the most dominant classes, is no longer learned.


3. Effect of Weighted Focal Loss
WFL combines two mechanisms:
Loss=α_c*(1−pt)^γ*log(pt)

where:
α_c introduces class weighting (as in WCE)
(1−pt)^γ emphasizes hard examples (as in FL)

This results in:
Down-weighting of frequent classes
Suppression of easy examples
Increased focus on difficult and underrepresented samples


4. Observed Learning Behavior
The combined effect of weighting and focal modulation leads to:
Strong suppression of dominant classes
Reduced learning signal for easy predictions
Concentration of learning on a limited subset of classes

In particular:
The model retains high accuracy for Class 7
However, it fails to learn other major classes such as Class 14
Rare classes are also not effectively recovered


5. Failure Mode
Unlike WCE and FL, WFL exhibits a distinct failure mode:
Over-suppression of dominant classes

Because:
Class weights reduce the importance of frequent classes
Focal loss further reduces the contribution of easy predictions

This results in:
Insufficient gradient signal for dominant classes
Collapse toward predicting a narrow subset of classes


6. Metric Interpretation

Despite strong IoU and F1 scores, class-wise results reveal:
Limited class diversity in predictions
High performance concentrated on a small number of classes

This suggests that:
Aggregate metrics may overestimate performance under WFL
as they are influenced by the subset of predicted classes.


7. Comparison with Other Loss Functions
Compared to Cross-Entropy:
Avoids trivial collapse to a single dominant class
Maintains higher IoU and F1

Compared to Weighted Cross-Entropy:
Fails to recover rare classes effectively
Suppresses even moderately frequent classes

Compared to Focal Loss:
Does not retain strong learning of hard classes (e.g., Class 13)
Introduces instability due to combined weighting effects


8. Key Insight
The results demonstrate:
Weighted Focal Loss may over-correct class imbalance by simultaneously down-weighting frequent classes and suppressing easy samples, leading to under-learning of even dominant classes.


9. Limitations
Loss of dominant class representation
No meaningful recovery of rare classes
Reduced class diversity in predictions
Sensitivity to hyperparameters (γ and α)


10. Conclusion
Weighted Focal Loss provides strong overall metrics but introduces instability in class-wise learning:
Maintains competitive IoU and F1 scores
Fails to learn key dominant and rare classes
Demonstrates over-suppression effects due to combined mechanisms

This suggests that:
Careful calibration is required when combining class weighting with focal modulation, as naive combination may degrade class-wise performance.
